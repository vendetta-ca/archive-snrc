Day 22 aboard. The light here does something strange to time — it has been almost continuously pale since we crossed 75° north, and I find myself losing track of hours in a way that no amount of watch-discipline corrects for. Eriksson thinks it's funny. I don't.

We deployed the passive array at 0600. By 0840 we had already collected more anomalous data than the entire previous expedition. I don't know what to make of that. Coordinator ████████ wants a preliminary write-up by tomorrow; I have told him that a preliminary write-up on data I don't understand is indistinguishable from fiction. He did not find this funny either.

At 14:17 local I was on the stern deck when I noticed the water had gone entirely flat. No wind — that part is normal up here sometimes — but the quality of the stillness was wrong. Flat water moves, even without wind. This wasn't moving. Eriksson came out to smoke and I pointed it out to him and he stood there for a while and then went back inside without saying anything.

The array recorded something at 14:22. I don't have the analysis back yet but the raw waveform on the monitor looked nothing like ice noise or biologics. It looked — and I am aware of how this will read in a formal report — deliberate. Structured. As if something below was choosing what to say and how to say it.

I've been doing this work for eleven years. I know what pareidolia is. I know that the human brain finds patterns in noise because patterns kept us alive on the savannah and we are a long way from having evolved out of that. I am writing this down anyway because I was trained to write down what I observe, and what I observed was that the waveform on that monitor looked like something that knew we were listening.

— I. Halvorsen, 2031-04-11, 23:04 UTC
