Day 22 aboard. The light here does something to time that I haven't found a good way to describe in any of the three previous entries and I'm not going to keep trying. We've been past 75° north long enough that my body has stopped expecting dark and has started just quietly accepting whatever the sky is doing. Eriksson thinks it's funny. He's been up here seven times.

We deployed the passive array at 06:00. Standard calibration run, standard broadcast schedule. By 08:40 we had more flagged signals than the previous expedition produced in nine days. I asked if we'd had a hardware change. We hadn't.

At 14:17 I was on the stern deck. The water had gone flat. I mean this in a way that is difficult to describe precisely. Eriksson came out and I pointed and he stood there for a while and went back inside without saying anything.

The array registered something at 14:22. I don't have the analysis back yet. The raw waveform on the monitor looked nothing like ice noise and nothing like biologics. There was internal structure to it. I wrote this down because field journals are for writing down what you observe, and this is what I observed.

The calibration broadcast went out at 14:00. The signal came in at 14:22. I am noting the times here as a factual record. I am not drawing a conclusion from them in this entry. The Classification Review Board will draw whatever conclusion the data supports.

I am aware of how the waveform description will read. The coordinator will flag it. The flag is correct procedure. I would apply it myself.
